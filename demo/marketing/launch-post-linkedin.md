## Draft A: punchy

I built a career agency today. The staff are agents. The first client is me.

At the Hermes Buildathon in SF, in one 8-hour sprint, I shipped Career Computa: an agent crew that runs a real job search end to end.

What it does for every job, autonomously:
- Pulls live postings straight from company job boards (Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby)
- Scores fit against my real resume, with the caveats written out, never a bare number
- Researches the company with live search, every claim linked to a source
- Tailors a resume variant through six quality gates
- Writes the LinkedIn connection note (under 300 characters) and the DM draft
- Delivers the finished package to my Telegram

What stays human: the send. I paste the note into LinkedIn myself. That is deliberate policy, not a capability gap.

The mechanic that makes it a product and not a script: trust graduation. Every draft type has a trust meter. Approve without edits enough times in a row and the review step disappears for that type. Edit once and the streak resets. Undo anytime. You literally watch the tap disappear as the agent earns it.

## Draft B: story

I am job hunting right now. Not hypothetically. So when the Hermes Buildathon kicked off this morning, I picked the problem I actually have.

The manual job search is a volume problem wearing a judgment costume. Scanning boards, reading JDs, rewriting resume bullets, drafting outreach: that is volume work. Deciding whether to hit send: that is judgment. Today I split those two apart.

Career Computa is a career agency run by agents. I onboarded through the same flow any stranger uses: uploaded my LinkedIn export and resume, confirmed my goals by voice, and the agency got to work on my real search.

A manager agent plans each task and delegates to specialists: a scout watching live company job boards, a researcher building sourced briefs, a drafter tailoring resume variants and writing outreach. The manager reviews their output against a hard checklist and sends weak work back for revision. I can read every trace, step by step, with the cost of each step attached.

What reaches me is a finished, apply-ready package. My only job is the send decision inside LinkedIn, and even that is designed to fade: every draft type carries a trust meter, and a streak of clean approvals graduates it to ship without review. One edit resets the streak. One click revokes graduation. Trust is earned, visible, and reversible, which is exactly how I would want to manage a human team too.
